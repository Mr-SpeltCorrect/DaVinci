THE CODICES

A Universe on Paper

Leonardo kept notebooks throughout his adult life. He wrote in mirror script — from right to left — in a hand so personal that scholars have debated whether he was deliberately trying to protect his ideas or simply found it more comfortable as a left-hander. The notebooks were not intended for publication; they were working documents, dense with observation, speculation, and invention.

After Leonardo's death in 1519, his student Francesco Melzi worked to preserve the notebooks. They were eventually dispersed across Europe; today they are held in institutions including the Royal Collection at Windsor Castle, the Biblioteca Ambrosiana in Milan (Codex Atlanticus — the largest, with over 1,100 pages), the British Library, and the Institut de France. Bill Gates purchased one notebook — the Codex Leicester — for $30.8 million in 1994 (Heydenreich).

SUBJECTS COVERED

The Range of His Curiosity

The notebooks address an astonishing range of subjects: anatomy (over 240 detailed drawings of the human body, including the first accurate depictions of the heart and its valves), geology, hydrology, botany, optics, mathematics, military engineering, architecture, music, philosophy, and the theory and practice of painting.

Leonardo approached all of these through direct observation and experiment — recording what he saw with his eyes rather than deferring to received classical authority. This empirical, hands-on attitude to knowledge made him a forerunner of the scientific method, even though he never systematized his findings or published a treatise (Bambach; Heydenreich).

"All our knowledge has its origins in our perceptions."

TECHNICAL DRAWINGS

Engineering Sketches as Art

Perhaps nowhere are the notebooks more spectacular than in their engineering drawings. From intricate pulley and crane designs to studies of water flow and turbulence, Leonardo rendered mechanical systems with a clarity and three-dimensional exactness that was unprecedented. His drawings use cross-section, exploded-view, and multiple-angle perspectives — techniques now standard in technical illustration but which Leonardo appears to have developed himself.

The Wikimedia Commons collection of Leonardo's technical drawings preserves many of these original sketches, allowing viewers to trace the evolution of his ideas from rough sketch to refined design. Subjects include: pulley mechanisms, crane designs, inflatable lifebelts, aqueducts, canal lock designs, and military devices of all kinds (Technical Drawings — Wikimedia Commons).
