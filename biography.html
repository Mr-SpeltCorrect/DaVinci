Biography

The extraordinary life of Leonardo da Vinci, 1452–1519

1452

Born on April 15 in Vinci, a small town in the Florentine territory of Tuscany.

c. 1466

Apprenticed to the Florentine artist Andrea del Verrocchio, one of the most accomplished painters and sculptors of the era.

1478

Accepted his first independent commission — an altarpiece for the chapel of San Bernardo in the Palazzo della Signoria — marking his emergence as an independent master.

1482

Moved to Milan at the invitation of Ludovico Sforza, where he would spend nearly two decades serving as court artist, engineer, and pageant organizer.

1495–1498

Painted The Last Supper on the refectory wall of Santa Maria delle Grazie in Milan.

1503–1519

Worked intermittently on the Mona Lisa in Florence, widely considered the most famous portrait ever painted.

1516

Accepted King Francis I of France's invitation to serve as premier painter, engineer, and architect of the king, spending his final years at the Château du Clos Lucé near Amboise.

1519

Died on May 2 at Amboise, aged 67, reportedly in the arms of the king. He gave his notebooks and drawings to his pupil Francesco Melzi.

EARLY LIFE & APPRENTICESHIP

Vinci and Florence

Leonardo's upbringing was unconventional. Raised initially by his mother and then by his father and paternal grandparents, he received a unique education, learning Latin, geometry, and mathematics. Recognizing Da Vinci's brillience, his father arranged for him to apprentice with Andrea del Verrocchio in Florence around 1466.

Leonardo trained alongside other talented young artists and mastered painting and sculpting. He thrived in that environment and grew his skillset massively.

MILAN & THE SFORZA COURT

Court Artist and Engineer

In 1482, Leonardo wrote to Ludovico Sforza offering his services primarily as a military engineer and architect. At the Sforza court, he oversaw festivals and theatrical productions, designed weapons and fortifications, studied anatomy and optics, and filled notebooks with his observations.

It was here that he painted Lady with an Ermine (c. 1489–90) and began work on the monumental Last Supper, completed around 1498.

FINAL YEARS

France and Legacy

After the fall of Milan to French forces in 1499, Leonardo moved between Florence, Venice, Rome, and back to Milan, always pursuing new intellectual and artistic projects. He studied cadavers to grow his knowledge in anatomy and created detailed drawings of the human body.

King Francis I invited Leonardo to move to France in 1516, where he spent his last three years. Though his physical capacities were declining he continued to plan, to write, and to draw until the end. He died on May 2, 1519, leaving behind thousands of pages of notes and drawings that would influence artists, scientists, and engineers for centuries to come.
